Idea For Casino Games Where The Player Almost Always Win

2020-10-29
  • Implications For Gambling

I have been coleading an internal study group going on Pricing Strategy. We just finished week 4 of a Coursera course Customer Value in Pricing Strategy devoted entirely to the various conditions under which people behave ‘irrationally’1 and how/when to use these conditions2 to demand a higher price on your product. While going through the materials I had an idea for a new casino game.

There are a few psychological phenomena from the course that are important primers:

I. People tend to be risk seeking when facing losses but risk averse when facing gains.

The example they give in the course is that faced with the scenario below (where each option has the same expected value), people will tend to go for the sure thing to gain the $50.

# Image of risk seeking

However in the case of facing a loss, they will tend to prefer testing their chances and taking the riskier option of a 50% chance of losing $100.

# image of risk avoiding

Another psychological phenomena described is:

  1. People tend to prefer disaggregated gains but aggregated losses

People prefer multiple small good things happening to them over just a single good thing occurring (even if the total value of events is equal). For example, people will prefer winning two small scratch cards over winning a single large scratch card:

# example scratch card

However people prefer taking bad events in aggregated forms. For example they prefer receiving one large bill over receiving multiple smaller bills:

# example bills

Even though the value of these events is equal3.

Implications For Gambling

Casinos are master psychological manipulators. However many lotteries, slots, and other games run counter to these particular phenomena. They offer repeated gauranteed initial losses (the quarter you put into the machine, the scratch ticket you buy from the counter, etc.) for the chance at a large payoff (winning the jackpot)4.

I thought an interesting set-up for a game would be one that flipped this and made the player very likely to win a small amount each time they played but that always carried the risk of a single large loss5.

As an example, imagine a roullette-like wheel numbered from 1 to 30. You are instructed to pick a number and as long as you don’t land on the selected number, you win $5 but if you do land on it you lose $1506. This particular game and bet would yield the casino an expected value of a little under 17 cents7. It also yields the player an expected 30 wins in a row8. The advert for the game might focus on the idea of “Tempting Fate.”9

You might course mix and match the ideas of a ‘big loss’ and a ‘big win’. Carrying on from the previous example the board (numbered 1:30) could be set-up where the player picks a big win spot ($150 gain), two big losing spots ($150 loss for either), and the rest all result in small wins ($5), the expected value for the casino is now $0.50.

There are a variety of forms this game could take (it doesn’t have to be a roullette-like wheel) the novel phenomena is to focus on ensuring players get repeated small wins while having them pay their losses in one-off large chunks.
